Additional system layer
Fact-FINDER — effort on top of CMS

Fact-FINDER is our product search, navigation, and discovery platform — not a CMS. Its effort does not replace CMS work; it is always additive. Any deployment touching search results, category pages, or campaign slots requires Fact-FINDER configuration in addition to the CMS task.

+
When a task involves both a CMS and Fact-FINDER, the subsidiary review is shared — no double counting. Add +1 day to the CMS feedback loop to cover the Fact-FINDER-specific coordination. For Fact-FINDER-only tasks, a full 4-day review window applies independently.

Analysis
What the numbers are telling us
Structural issue
Feedback loops are the invisible budget
The 4-day feedback window per market is the largest single controllable variable in our delivery timelines. At 12 markets, that's 48 days of overhead per content type — before any IT issue occurs. This is not captured in base estimates and rarely appears in project plans, but it is the most consistent driver of delivery overrun.
Planning risk
The "× markets" line is where roadmaps fail
A replicated page with updates appears manageable at 2–4 days. The moment you multiply by 12 markets with the 4-day feedback loop, you're looking at 72–96 days (OT) or 72–84 days (FS) — on a single content type. Campaign launches requiring simultaneous rollout across markets compound this further, creating coordination pressure that is never visible in initial estimates.
Opportunity
FirstSpirit's real advantage is in replication
For original page creation, the CMS ranges overlap significantly (2–5d FS vs 4–5d OT). The meaningful gap is in replicated content — the task type that scales. FS is 30–50% faster on replicated pages without updates (1–2d vs 2–3d), which at volume represents a substantial cumulative saving. Architecture changes are also faster, with a meaningful downstream unblocking effect.
Industry gap
Both systems lag the market standard
Modern headless CMS platforms (Contentful, Storyblok, Sanity) benchmark at 0.5–1 day for replicated pages. Mid-tier enterprise platforms like AEM clock in at 1–2 days. Both Häfele systems are 2–3× slower on replication. This is a system architecture constraint, not a team performance issue, and should inform the medium-term platform roadmap.
Fact-FINDER layer
Campaigns and category work carry a hidden double effort
Every content deployment that touches product discovery — campaigns, category pages, search-driven landing pages — requires Fact-FINDER configuration on top of the CMS work. A campaign rollout across 12 markets doesn't just multiply the CMS estimate; it multiplies the combined CMS + Fact-FINDER estimate. The shared feedback loop (4 days + 1 day for FF coordination) applies to both in parallel, but the base configuration work is always additional. This compounding effect is one of the most consistent sources of delivery overrun and needs to be a line item in any rollout plan involving search or product discovery.
What we need
Our asks from this analysis
01
Align planning on realistic timelines, not base estimates
We need our roadmaps and resourcing conversations to reflect what delivery actually takes — not the base figures. The realistic column in this document (base + 4-day feedback loop) should become our internal planning standard. This protects the team and sets expectations our 2 FTEs can actually meet.
02
Bring our subsidiaries closer to the delivery reality
The 4-day feedback window is our planning baseline, but in practice it is often exceeded — and the teams on the other side are rarely aware of the knock-on effect. We're not asking for rigid rules, but for a shared understanding: helping subsidiary teams see what a delayed review actually costs downstream, so faster turnarounds become a natural priority rather than an imposed one.
03
Accelerate FirstSpirit adoption for replication-heavy markets
Our own data shows FirstSpirit is 30–50% faster on replicated content — the task we do most. We want to prioritise migration for the markets where replication volume is highest, so we're capturing those efficiency gains where they compound most. We need resourcing and sign-off to sequence this properly.
04
Put the platform question on the strategic agenda
We're operating at 2–3× the replication speed of modern CMS platforms — and that gap is a system constraint, not a team one. We're not raising this as an immediate action, but we believe it needs a dedicated conversation at the right level: what would a future platform look like, what would migration cost, and what would we gain? We'd like to be part of that discussion.
